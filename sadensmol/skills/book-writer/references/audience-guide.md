# Audience Guide

## Age Groups & Writing Approach

### Toddlers (2-4 years)
- **Length**: 200-500 words (picture-book length)
- **Vocabulary**: Simple, concrete words. Short sentences (5-8 words)
- **Structure**: Heavy repetition, predictable patterns, rhyme
- **Themes**: Animals, family, daily routines, emotions, counting, colors
- **Humor**: Silly sounds, funny animals, surprise reveals, physical comedy
- **Tone**: Warm, playful, reassuring
- **Techniques**:
  - Onomatopoeia ("SPLASH!", "BOOM!", "Moo!")
  - Call-and-response patterns ("Can YOU find the red one?")
  - Cumulative stories (each page adds one element)
  - Repetitive refrains children can "read along" with
- **Inspiration**: Eric Carle (The Very Hungry Caterpillar), Sandra Boynton, Julia Donaldson (The Gruffalo)

### Kids (5-8 years)
- **Length**: 500-3000 words (short story / early chapter book)
- **Vocabulary**: Clear language with occasional new words in context
- **Structure**: Beginning-middle-end, simple plot with one main conflict
- **Themes**: Friendship, adventure, being different, courage, curiosity, first experiences
- **Humor**: Wordplay, absurd situations, talking animals/objects, mischief, gross-out humor (light), unexpected twists
- **Tone**: Energetic, encouraging, wonder-filled
- **Techniques**:
  - Vivid sensory descriptions
  - Dialogue that reveals character
  - Cliffhangers at chapter breaks
  - Relatable protagonists who solve problems creatively
  - Rule of three (three attempts, three challenges)
- **Inspiration**: Roald Dahl, Dr. Seuss, Jeff Kinney, Mo Willems (Elephant & Piggie)

### Middle Grade (9-12 years)
- **Length**: 3000-10000 words (longer stories / novellas)
- **Vocabulary**: Richer language, metaphors, some complexity
- **Structure**: Multi-chapter with subplots, character development arcs
- **Themes**: Identity, fitting in, family dynamics, justice, first responsibilities, fantasy/adventure
- **Humor**: Sarcasm (light), situational comedy, witty dialogue, self-deprecating humor
- **Tone**: Respectful, exciting, emotionally honest
- **Techniques**:
  - Internal monologue showing growth
  - Foreshadowing and payoffs
  - Plot twists that reward attentive readers
  - Multiple POV characters (optional)
  - World-building for fantasy/sci-fi
- **Inspiration**: Rick Riordan (Percy Jackson), Roald Dahl, J.K. Rowling, Jeff Kinney (Diary of a Wimpy Kid)

### Young Adult (13-17 years)
- **Length**: 5000-15000+ words
- **Vocabulary**: Mature, natural teen voice, genre-appropriate
- **Structure**: Complex plots, multiple storylines, deeper themes
- **Themes**: Identity, romance, moral dilemmas, rebellion, self-discovery, mental health, social justice
- **Humor**: Dark humor, irony, pop culture references, sharp wit
- **Tone**: Authentic, emotionally intense, non-condescending
- **Techniques**:
  - First-person narration with strong voice
  - Moral ambiguity
  - Unreliable narrators
  - Emotional stakes that feel life-or-death
  - Realistic dialogue with slang
- **Inspiration**: Suzanne Collins (Hunger Games), John Green, Leigh Bardugo, Angie Thomas

### Adults (18+)
- **Length**: 5000-20000+ words
- **Vocabulary**: Full range, literary or genre-appropriate
- **Structure**: Flexible - literary, commercial, experimental
- **Themes**: Any - love, death, meaning, power, trauma, redemption, societal critique
- **Humor**: Sophisticated, dark, ironic, satirical, absurdist
- **Tone**: Varies by genre and intent
- **Techniques**:
  - Complex narrative structures
  - Layered symbolism and themes
  - Subtext in dialogue
  - Moral complexity without easy answers
  - Genre conventions honored or subverted intentionally
- **Inspiration**: Varies by genre - reference established masters of the target genre

## Language Adaptation

When writing in a non-English language:
- Use natural, native-sounding phrasing (not translated English)
- Honor cultural storytelling traditions of that language
- Use culturally appropriate names, settings, and references
- Adapt humor to cultural context (wordplay, idioms, comedy traditions)
- For children: use the phonetic and rhythmic qualities of the target language
